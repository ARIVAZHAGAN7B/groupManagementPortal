CORE MODULE WRITE-UP: GROUP MANAGEMENT AND MEMBERSHIP MANAGEMENT

1. Introduction to the Core Module

Group management and membership management form the operational heart of the Group Management Portal. These two modules are responsible for organizing students into structured groups, controlling how students enter or leave those groups, maintaining leadership assignments, enforcing institutional rules, and keeping group status synchronized with policy conditions. In this project, a group is not treated as a simple collection of students. Instead, it is modeled as a governed unit with its own identity, tier, status, member capacity, leadership structure, rank rules, and lifecycle controls. The membership module then acts as the transactional layer that records every student-to-group association, role assignment, role change, rank update, entry event, exit event, and review-driven progression.

From the database perspective, the `sgroup` table stores the master record of each group, while the `memberships` table functions as the bridge between students and groups. This design makes the solution flexible and auditable because the group remains a stable entity even when members change over time, and membership history can be preserved instead of being overwritten. The module further extends this core by adding `join_requests` for student applications to groups and `leadership_role_requests` for controlled promotion into leadership positions. Together, these tables and services support a governed workflow rather than an uncontrolled student self-assignment system.

The implementation reflects a rule-based academic environment. A group can exist in multiple states such as `INACTIVE`, `ACTIVE`, `FROZEN`, and `ARCHIVED`. Group activation is not arbitrary. It depends on the operational policy maintained in the system settings, especially the minimum and maximum group size constraints and whether leadership positions must be filled before activation. By default, the system is configured with a minimum of 9 members and a maximum of 11 members, and leadership is required for activation. This means the platform enforces meaningful group composition before a group becomes fully operational.

This module is important because it converts institutional group policies into executable business logic. Instead of relying on manual record keeping, paper approvals, or informal member coordination, the portal formalizes every step using APIs, validation rules, database constraints, and role-based authorization. As a result, group formation, group stability, leadership integrity, and student movement are all managed through a consistent digital process. For a real-world college or departmental environment, this directly improves fairness, traceability, and administrative efficiency.

2. Group Management Details

The group management module is responsible for creating, listing, updating, activating, freezing, and switching groups. Each group is defined by a unique `group_code`, a `group_name`, a `tier`, and a `status`. The supported tier values are `D`, `C`, `B`, and `A`, which allow the institution to classify groups according to performance, maturity, or internal policy rules. The supported status values are `ACTIVE`, `INACTIVE`, `FROZEN`, and `ARCHIVED`. This status model is central to the module because it determines whether a group is open for membership activities and whether it can participate normally in the system.

Group creation is role-controlled. Administrators and system administrators can create groups directly, while student-side creation is only permitted when the operational policy explicitly enables it. This is an important governance feature because it allows the institution to decide whether group formation should be centralized or student-initiated. When a new group is created, the system records its basic identity and normally initializes it in the `INACTIVE` state. This approach is more reliable than activating the group immediately, because it allows leadership roles and membership conditions to be completed before the group becomes operational.

The group listing functionality is more than a simple table fetch. The backend enriches each group with discovery-oriented fields such as active member count, captain name, captain points, total points, lifetime base points, eligibility bonus points, lifetime total points, number of vacancies, whether the group is accepting applications, and the current phase eligibility status. These computed fields make the group catalogue useful for both students and administrators. A student can quickly identify a group that still has space, while an administrator can understand performance and compliance at a glance.

The group search and filtering workflow also supports practical decision-making. Groups can be filtered by tier, status, captain name, whether they are accepting applications, vacancy availability, minimum points, and current phase eligibility status. This helps transform the platform from a static data store into a decision-support system. For example, a student who wants to join a group can filter to only active groups with available vacancies, while an administrator can analyze only frozen groups or only those failing eligibility in the current phase.

Another major feature is controlled group activation. A group is activated only when it satisfies the current policy. The system checks the number of active members and verifies whether the required leadership roles are filled. The leadership roles considered essential are `CAPTAIN`, `VICE_CAPTAIN`, `STRATEGIST`, and `MANAGER`. If the group size is outside the allowed range or if leadership is incomplete, activation is blocked unless an administrator uses an override. This rule gives the platform a strong governance model. It prevents partially formed or structurally weak groups from being treated as fully valid operational entities.

Freezing a group is another important administrative control. A frozen group remains in the database, but it is effectively locked from normal membership growth and leadership approval flows. Students cannot apply to a frozen group, cannot directly join it, and cannot switch into it. Leadership requests for frozen groups are also blocked. This is useful when a group is under review, disciplinary hold, or administrative transition. Instead of deleting data, the platform temporarily suspends operations while preserving the group record and history.

The module also supports a soft delete pattern. Deleting a group does not permanently remove it from the database. Instead, the implementation marks the group as `INACTIVE`. This is a safer and more enterprise-oriented design because it preserves referential integrity and historical records. Since memberships, points, requests, and audits may already be associated with the group, a soft delete prevents accidental data loss and keeps the system analytically consistent.

Group switching is handled as a controlled transition rather than a casual update. A student can switch from one group to another only on the current phase's Change Day. This rule is enforced by the backend service using the phase configuration. During the transaction, the system marks the previous membership as `LEFT`, checks vacancy rules in the destination group, blocks switching into frozen groups, inserts a fresh membership entry in the new group, and recalculates the status of both the old and the new group. This process maintains consistency for both student history and group composition. It also supports an incubation period, which is derived from system policy and recorded as `incubation_end_date` in the new membership.

3. Membership Management Details

If group management defines the container, membership management defines participation. The `memberships` table stores each student's association with a group, their functional role inside that group, their current membership status, their join date, leave date, incubation end date, and any rank override that may have been manually assigned. This design allows the system to record both the current state and the historical path of a student across groups.

Each membership belongs to exactly one student and one group, and the system enforces these relationships through foreign keys. The supported roles are `CAPTAIN`, `VICE_CAPTAIN`, `STRATEGIST`, `MANAGER`, and `MEMBER`. The supported membership states are `ACTIVE` and `LEFT`. A key rule in the business logic is that a student can have only one active group membership at a time. This is checked before a new membership is created. If an active membership already exists, the join attempt is rejected. This prevents duplicate participation and preserves group accountability.

Direct joining into a group is supported through a controlled service. Before a student is added to a group, the backend validates that the role is valid, checks whether the student already belongs to another group, verifies that the rejoin deadline is still valid if the student had recently left a group, confirms that the target group exists and is not frozen, checks that the group has not reached maximum capacity, and ensures that leadership roles are not duplicated. For example, a group cannot have two active captains or two active managers at the same time. Once a new membership is created, the system immediately recalculates the group's status based on its new member count.

Leaving a group is also carefully governed. The platform can enforce a Change Day rule for leaving, depending on system policy. By default, that enforcement is enabled. This means a student is normally allowed to leave a group only on the designated change day of the active phase. After the leave transaction, the membership is marked as `LEFT`, the leave date is stored, the group member count is recalculated, and the group status is updated accordingly. The response also includes rejoin deadline information because the system imposes a time-bound rule on how long a student can remain ungrouped without administrative intervention.

One of the more distinctive membership features is the rejoin deadline policy. The logic states that once a student leaves a group, the student must rejoin by the end of the next working day. The module calculates this deadline while excluding weekends and then compares it with the current time. If the deadline is missed, the student can no longer rejoin freely and administrative approval becomes necessary. This rule is valuable in an institutional setting because it discourages prolonged unassigned status and keeps the overall group ecosystem stable.

The module also supports administrative and captain-level member removal. An administrator can remove an active membership with policy override capabilities, while a captain can remove another member of the same group, provided the captain is not trying to remove themself. A removal reason is mandatory, which strengthens traceability and discourages arbitrary decisions. When a removal occurs, the implementation again uses the leave workflow so that status recalculation, leave timestamps, and downstream rules remain consistent.

The "my group" feature gives a student a reliable view of their current active membership along with its associated group details. On the administrative side, the module exposes a full membership listing service with filtering by status, group, student, and role. This is useful for review, auditing, and analytics. Because the backend supports pagination, the module remains scalable even when the number of stored memberships becomes large.

4. Join Requests, Leadership Control, and Rank Management

The project does not rely only on direct membership operations. It also introduces a formal request workflow through join requests and leadership role requests. This makes the platform more realistic and suitable for controlled environments where every student movement may require review.

The join request subsystem allows a student to apply for a group instead of joining it instantly. Before a request is created, the system checks that the student is not already an active member of another group, confirms the target group exists, blocks applications to frozen groups, checks whether the group still has vacancies, and prevents duplicate pending requests by the same student for the same group. The resulting request enters the `PENDING` state. This workflow is useful when the organization wants the captain or administrators to evaluate admissions rather than allowing unrestricted entry.

Approval and rejection of join requests follow a role-aware model. Group captains and administrators can review pending requests, but only administrators can approve a student directly into a leadership role through this path. If a request is approved, the service performs a transactional sequence: it verifies the student's current membership state again, validates vacancy and freeze rules again, enforces the rejoin deadline rule, checks leadership uniqueness when a leadership role is involved, creates the new membership, and recalculates the group status. This repeat validation inside the transaction is critical because it protects the system from race conditions and stale UI decisions.

Leadership role requests add another layer of structured governance. A student who is already an active member of a group may request a promotion into one of the leadership roles: `CAPTAIN`, `VICE_CAPTAIN`, `STRATEGIST`, or `MANAGER`. However, the member must currently be a normal `MEMBER`, not already a leadership holder. The group must not be frozen, and the requested role must still be vacant. The system also blocks duplicate pending leadership requests from the same member for the same group. This reflects a disciplined organizational model in which leadership is earned through a defined request and approval process rather than assigned informally.

Leadership request approval is restricted to administrators and system administrators. When approved, the system confirms that the membership still exists, is still active, still matches the request, and that the requested role is still vacant. It then updates the membership role and refreshes the group status. This matters because some groups may move from `INACTIVE` to `ACTIVE` once all required leadership positions are filled. The module also exposes administrative notifications such as the number of pending leadership requests and groups with missing leadership roles, making the system proactive rather than purely reactive.

Another advanced feature of membership management is member rank handling. The system supports both automated review-based ranking and manual rank override. Ranks range from 1 to 5, with 1 being the highest. The review cycle occurs every 5 completed phases. During review, the platform evaluates members using three criteria: `LOYALTY`, `CONTRIBUTION`, and `RELIABILITY`. Default thresholds and score weights are stored in the database and can be overridden at the group level. This allows the same ranking framework to adapt to different group cultures or performance expectations.

The ranking model is intentionally limited so that top positions remain meaningful. For ranks 1 through 4, only two members can occupy each rank within a group, while remaining members fall into rank 5. The review logic compares candidates based on total score and then resolves ties using contribution, reliability, loyalty, and join date. The result is a transparent, rules-based performance system. In addition, captains and administrators can manually override a member's rank. Captains have an extra restriction: they can override ranks only when at least three active leadership roles are filled in the group. This prevents rank manipulation in structurally incomplete groups.

5. Functional Importance, Data Flow, and Report-Level Summary

From a systems design perspective, group management and membership management are tightly connected modules that model the full lifecycle of collective participation. A group is created, populated, validated, activated, monitored, and occasionally frozen. Membership records are created, updated, transferred, ranked, and archived through `LEFT` state transitions. The combination of these two modules ensures that the platform always knows which student belongs where, under what role, under what conditions, and with what permissions.

The data flow begins with core identity tables such as `users`, `students`, and `admins`. Group records are then stored in `sgroup`, while operational policies are fetched from `system_settings`. A student may discover a group through the listing API, submit a join request, receive approval, and become an active member through an inserted membership row. As the student contributes over time, their membership may be reviewed for rank, or they may apply for leadership. If the student leaves or switches groups, the original membership is preserved historically as `LEFT`, and a new membership record is created for the next phase of participation. This design gives the project a strong audit trail and preserves longitudinal data for later reporting and analysis.

The module also reflects solid software engineering practice. It uses role-based authorization, policy-driven validation, transaction-based updates for sensitive operations, foreign key relationships for integrity, computed metrics for discovery, and soft status transitions instead of destructive deletion. These design choices make the system safer, easier to audit, and better aligned with real organizational operations. Instead of hardcoding everything into the UI, the business rules are enforced at the backend service layer, which improves consistency across all clients.

In practical terms, this module solves several recurring administrative problems. It prevents students from joining multiple groups at once. It avoids overfilling groups beyond policy limits. It ensures leadership uniqueness within a group. It restricts sensitive actions like role change, rank override, and member removal to authorized actors. It prevents free movement outside controlled time windows such as Change Day. It also keeps group status aligned with real member count and leadership readiness. These are exactly the types of controls that are difficult to maintain manually but straightforward when encoded into a proper portal.

For the project report, this module can be presented as one of the strongest examples of domain-driven implementation. It does not simply store student and group names. It captures the logic of formation, eligibility, responsibility, review, and transition. The module supports fairness through transparent rules, accountability through auditability, flexibility through configurable policies, and scalability through normalized schema design. Because of this, group management and membership management together can be considered the core coordination engine of the entire application.

In summary, the group management module defines the structural and policy framework of the system, while the membership management module governs student participation inside that framework. Their integration creates a robust platform for managing group-based academic workflows. The modules support creation, discovery, approval, role assignment, ranking, activation, freezing, leaving, and switching, all under policy control. This makes the Group Management Portal not just a CRUD application, but a rule-governed management system capable of supporting real institutional operations with consistency and traceability.
